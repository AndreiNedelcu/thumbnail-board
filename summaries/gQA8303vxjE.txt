# Summary

The Millau Viaduct in France is the world's tallest bridge, soaring 340 meters above the Tarn Valley and spanning 2.4 kilometers. Built to solve decades of severe traffic congestion in central France's challenging terrain, the bridge was initially deemed impossible by critics. Engineer Michel Vero pioneered the bold concept of spanning directly across the valley rather than descending into it, while renowned architect Lord Norman Foster joined the project to ensure the structure enhanced rather than damaged the landscape. Foster's design innovations included reducing the number of towers from nine to seven, creating faceted tower columns that resembled tuning forks to minimize visual impact and withstand wind speeds exceeding 130 mph, and choosing gray coloring to blend with the sky rather than the valley. The deck, prefabricated at the Eiffel Factory, was slid across the completed towers using hydraulic systems with millimeter precision. Completed in 2004, the viaduct demonstrates the harmony between engineering and architecture, becoming an iconic landmark and tourist destination that has economically revitalized the region while reducing carbon emissions. The video, featuring interviews with Lord Foster, celebrates how collaborative design between different disciplines can achieve infrastructure that is simultaneously functional and beautiful.
