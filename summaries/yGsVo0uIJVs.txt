In 1968, the Soviet ballistic missile submarine K-129 mysteriously sank in the Pacific Ocean northwest of Hawaii, prompting a covert Cold War operation that became one of the CIA's most audacious missions. After the U.S. Navy located the wreck at 5,000 meters depth, the agency devised an extraordinary plan to retrieve it: building a massive claw-equipped ship while maintaining a cover story. The CIA enlisted billionaire Howard Hughes to pose as a deep-sea mining entrepreneur, commissioning the Glomar Explorer—a specially engineered vessel featuring advanced positioning systems and a 5,000-meter-long steel pipe mechanism capable of reaching the ocean floor. In 1974, the risky operation proceeded despite Soviet surveillance and suspicion, successfully raising the submarine partially before a critical mechanical failure caused it to break apart, with most sinking back to the depths. The mission's exposure by the Los Angeles Times in 1975 forced the government to issue the famous "neither confirm nor deny" response, preventing any second recovery attempt. The video appeals to viewers interested in Cold War history, espionage operations, and engineering feats, detailing both the technological sophistication and dramatic geopolitical tensions underlying this classified operation.
