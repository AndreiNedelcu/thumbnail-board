This video examines several near-catastrophic aviation incidents at major U.S. airports, starting with Air Canada Flight 759's close call at San Francisco International Airport in 2017, where the aircraft nearly collided with four parked planes on a taxiway due to pilot confusion about runway closures and fatigue. The analysis then explores subsequent incidents including a Delta-American Airlines near-collision at JFK Airport where miscommunication between different control frequencies and unclear taxi routes nearly caused disaster, and a FedEx-Southwest incident in Austin where a cargo plane conducting an instrument landing almost struck a departing Southwest flight in dense fog. The video addresses systemic factors contributing to these incidents: fatigue, inadequate communication protocols, reliance on visual approaches without instrument confirmation, single controllers managing multiple frequencies during night hours, and crew confusion caused by non-standard procedures. It contextualizes these modern close calls within aviation history, referencing the 1977 Tenerife disaster—the deadliest aviation accident ever—involving two Boeing 747s colliding on a runway. The content targets aviation enthusiasts, safety professionals, and general audiences interested in understanding how procedural failures and human factors create dangerous situations, ultimately explaining reforms implemented to prevent future accidents and questioning whether current measures adequately address growing near-miss statistics.
