Richard Reeves, a senior fellow at the Brookings Institution, argues that boys and men face significant structural challenges across education, employment, and family structures that deserve serious policy attention. He presents data showing that girls now substantially outpace boys in educational metrics—including reading proficiency, high school GPA, college enrollment, and degree completion—a reversal from 50 years ago. Reeves attributes these disparities partly to neuroscientific differences in brain development timing, with girls' prefrontal cortexes maturing one to two years earlier than boys', advantaging them in an education system rewarding homework completion and long-term planning. He emphasizes that working-class boys and Black boys face amplified struggles and criticizes the political polarization preventing mature policy discussions. Reeves also addresses employment, noting most men today earn less than their counterparts in 1979, and critiques how diagnostic labeling may pathologize normal male behavior. He proposes concrete solutions: collecting disaggregated data by sex, delaying school entry for boys, increasing male teachers, and expanding vocational education. His book targets policymakers, educators, and general audiences seeking evidence-based solutions to gendered educational and economic inequality.
