India faces a severe employment crisis despite being the world's fastest-growing major economy, with joblessness potentially as high as 8% and youth unemployment reaching over 42% for those aged 20-24. The country's 1.4 billion population includes over half under age 30, yet lacks sufficient high-quality job creation to absorb this demographic advantage. Approximately 90% of India's workforce operates in the informal sector—farms, construction, and unskilled roles without regular salaries or benefits—while only 10% of 112 million jobs generated in the past decade were formal positions. A critical mismatch exists between education and employment: higher literacy paradoxically increases unemployment risk, as colleges deliver outdated curricula and lack practical skill training. Women face particular challenges, with India's female labor force participation among the world's lowest due to cultural expectations about caregiving roles; closing this gap could expand GDP by nearly a third by 2050. Manufacturing comprises only a fraction of GDP rather than the targeted 25%, as companies hesitate to invest due to red tape and regulatory barriers. Political consequences emerged when Prime Minister Modi's party lost its parliamentary majority partly due to unfulfilled job creation promises. Proposed solutions include manufacturing incentives, workforce training programs, simplified labor laws, and attracting foreign investment, though experts debate whether current measures sufficiently address this fundamental economic challenge threatening India's development trajectory.
