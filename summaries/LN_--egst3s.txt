# Summary

Dementia villages are purpose-built residential facilities that apply universal design principles to create therapeutic environments for people with severe dementia. The most well-known example is Hogeweyk in the Netherlands, which replaces the institutional nursing home model with a neighborhood structure containing small household units, public spaces like cafes and shops, and trained staff embedded throughout. Rather than relying solely on surface-level design modifications—such as consistent flooring and color-coded doors—dementia villages adopt a structural approach mimicking ordinary communities, allowing residents autonomy to navigate between private and public spaces while maintaining safety. This design philosophy recognizes dementia patients as people with agency and life histories deserving quality of life, not merely patients requiring medical management. Research suggests the model reduces psychiatric medications and behavioral issues, though evidence comparing it to traditional care remains limited. As global populations age and dementia cases increase, dementia villages represent an innovative but expensive approach to long-term care that demonstrates how thoughtfully designed environments can benefit both people with cognitive impairments and society more broadly through universal design principles.
