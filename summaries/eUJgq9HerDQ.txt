# Summary

This documentary explores Nauru, the world's least-visited independent nation located in the Pacific Ocean, accessible only through a grueling 48-hour journey involving multiple flights and layovers. Once a wealthy phosphate-mining economy that achieved the world's highest GDP per capita in 1975 following the discovery of guano-based phosphate reserves, Nauru has since become a cautionary tale of resource depletion and poor governance. The country now holds unfortunate distinctions as the world's most obese nation (60% obesity rate) and has the highest cigarette consumption globally, conditions exacerbated by 80% of the island becoming uninhabitable due to mining destruction and its resulting dependence on imported processed foods costing inflated prices. The video details Nauru's past prosperity when citizens became millionaires and purchased luxury vehicles for decoration, its subsequent economic collapse after phosphate reserves depleted, involvement in money-laundering scandals with organized crime, and its controversial agreement to host Australian refugee camps. Currently facing poverty affecting 52% of the population and planning controversial deep-sea mining operations, Nauru serves as a cautionary example of unsustainable resource extraction and mismanagement targeting audiences interested in geography, economics, and geopolitical issues.
