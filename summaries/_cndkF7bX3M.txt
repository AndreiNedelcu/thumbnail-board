# Summary

This documentary follows an explorer investigating peculiar forest patterns visible on Google Earth in Uruguay, particularly a formation shaped like a fingerprint. The investigation reveals that Uruguay's forests are predominantly industrial eucalyptus and pine plantations rather than natural ecosystems, established after a 1987 law incentivized afforestation to develop a forestry economy. The documentary discovers that these plantation forests, planted in distinctive contour-line patterns that follow terrain topography, are part of a massive pulp and paper production operation. Finnish company UPM owns roughly 30% of Uruguay's 1.1 million hectares of forest plantations and operates two of three major pulp mills within tax-free zones, exporting 4.8 million tons of pulp annually to Europe and China. The film examines the environmental and social consequences of industrial afforestation—including biodiversity loss, altered water cycles, soil degradation, habitat destruction, and community displacement caused by new infrastructure like the $1 billion railway connecting mills to ports. The documentary challenges the perception of afforestation as universally beneficial, arguing that replacing biodiverse grasslands with monoculture tree farms for global consumer demand creates ecological trade-offs often overlooked in discussions of reforestation. It targets audiences interested in environmental issues, geopolitics of resource extraction, and investigative journalism.
