# Operating Room Color History

This video traces the fascinating history of why operating rooms are painted green, challenging the assumption that it's simply a scientific fact. The narrator investigates how early surgical theaters were entirely white for perceived cleanliness, but surgeons struggled with glare from sunlit windows reflecting off white surfaces and walls, causing eye strain during procedures. The pivotal change came through Dr. Harry Mitchell Sherman, a prominent California surgeon who, in 1914, argued that green—the complementary color to red blood—should replace white sheeting. Sherman's reasoning blended practical observation with pseudoscientific theories about spinach chlorophyll, natural selection, and restful colors, yet his advocacy proved effective. Despite questionable scientific justifications compared to competing proposals like amber-tinted spectacles and beaked caps, Sherman's influence within the American College of Surgeons helped establish green as the standard. By the 1950s, green operating rooms became ubiquitous, and the trend extended to surgical scrubs. The video targets curious audiences interested in medical history and how practical solutions often triumph through persuasive advocacy rather than pure scientific rigor, illustrating how professional practices evolve through individual determination and institutional influence.
