# GoPro: From Billion-Dollar Success to Near Collapse

GoPro's journey exemplifies how a visionary product can create extraordinary wealth while demonstrating how poor strategic decisions can nearly destroy that success. Founded by Nick Woodman, who feared entering traditional employment, GoPro emerged from a surfing trip idea into a revolutionary action camera that captured user-generated content perfectly suited for viral sharing. The company achieved remarkable growth with minimal competition, peaking at $1.6 billion in revenue by 2015. However, after going public in 2014, Woodman's ambitions shifted dangerously. Attempting to transform GoPro into a media company rival to Netflix and Red Bull, the company spent hundreds of millions on original content productions that never launched, while simultaneously over-hiring and neglecting core camera innovation. The Hero 4 Session price miscalculation and the Karma drone disaster—which included a critical battery recall—accelerated the decline. Profitability collapsed from 8% margins to losses for five consecutive years. By refocusing on hardware and introducing a subscription-based business model (GoPro Plus), the company stabilized. However, facing new competitors like DJI and Insta360, plus manufacturing challenges and market contraction from 6.6 million units sold in 2015 to 3 million by 2023, GoPro's future remains uncertain despite recent recovery efforts.
