# Summary

This documentary follows honey hunters in Nepal's remote Himalayan villages who risk their lives harvesting mad honey, a rare hallucinogenic substance produced by the world's largest honeybees (Apis laboriosa). The honey contains potent grayana toxins derived from rhododendron nectar that can cause vivid hallucinations, paralysis, and severe physical effects. The film documents the Gurung tribe's ancient harvesting methods, unchanged for 7,500 years, which involve scaling 80-meter rope ladders down treacherous cliff faces while managing swarms of giant bees that deliver hundreds of stings. Historically used medicinally in small doses as an aphrodisiac and cure, the line between therapeutic benefit and toxicity is razor-thin. The documentary captures the entire process from preparation and ladder construction through extraction and storage, while also documenting the filmmaker's firsthand experience consuming the honey, which produces tingling, numbness, nausea, vomiting, and temperature regulation difficulties lasting hours. The video targets adventure and documentary enthusiasts interested in extreme harvesting practices, cultural anthropology, and ethnobotany, while highlighting how hunters earn less than $50 per kilogram despite the honey selling for approximately $400 per 200-gram portion.
