# Summary

This documentary explores Larınada, Peru's highest inhabited settlement at 5,100 meters altitude in the Andes, where approximately 50,000 people live with only 50% of sea-level oxygen. The residents—primarily migrants seeking gold wealth—have adapted biologically to produce double the normal blood cells, though newcomers face severe altitude sickness. The town presents extreme hardship: metal shacks without electricity or heating, frozen ground despite tropical proximity, no sewage system, and garbage-filled streets. Gold mining dominates the economy through an illegal, exploitative labor system called Kachoreo, where workers labor unpaid for a month, earning income only on a designated day based on their finds. Mining conditions are dangerously poor, with explosions and collapses occurring 25 times more frequently than in developed nations, yet compensation for deaths is merely $600. Environmental devastation from mercury and cyanide contamination results in a life expectancy of only 35 years. The documentary targets viewers interested in extreme living conditions, economic desperation, and the human cost of global commodity production, providing intimate interviews with miners and firsthand documentation of survival in one of Earth's most inhospitable inhabited locations.
