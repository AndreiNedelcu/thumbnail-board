This video traces the dramatic decline of US Steel from its mid-20th century dominance—when the company employed one percent of America's civilian workforce and produced 67% of global steel—to its current state of seeking acquisition by Japan's Nippon Steel for $15 billion. The narrative centers on how stubbornness and technological complacency, rather than external enemies like unions or foreign competition alone, caused the company's downfall. The creator explains the shift from massive, capital-intensive open-hearth furnaces in integrated mills to smaller, more efficient electric arc furnace mini-mills pioneered by competitors like Nucor. While US Steel dismissed mini-mills as incapable of producing high-quality sheet metal—the most profitable product—Nucor invested $250 million to solve this challenge, eventually cracking the market in the early 1990s. The video targets business-minded audiences interested in industrial history and corporate strategy, using personal storytelling and on-location visits to illustrate how technological innovation and market adaptation determine industry winners. The overarching lesson emphasizes how market leaders can lose dominance not through external forces but through failure to recognize internal technological threats.
