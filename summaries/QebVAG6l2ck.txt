# Summary

This video explores what happens when Stockfish, the world's strongest chess engine, is programmed to deliberately play the mathematically worst possible move at every turn against itself. The experiment, submitted by a viewer, creates an absurdist chess game filled with bizarre positions, blunders, and multiple instances where checkmate-in-one moves are available but deliberately ignored by both sides. The game features unusual tactics like intentional piece sacrifices, strategic king movements into the center of the board, knight underpromotions, and hanging pieces that neither side captures because capturing would be the optimal move rather than the worst one. The video targets chess enthusiasts and puzzle solvers interested in both serious analysis and comedic chess content. It showcases how an engine optimized for losing produces positions that violate basic chess principles, with the game ultimately lasting 112 moves as one side finally achieves checkmate after the other exhausts all avenues for self-sabotage. The presentation combines detailed move-by-move commentary with humor about the illogical positions and decision-making that emerge from this reverse-engineered gameplay.
