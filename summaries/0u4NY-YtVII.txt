# Chick Bank: The $130 Million Gaming Scam

In July 2016, a Turkish entrepreneur named Memed Iden launched Chick Bank, a deceptively simple farming game with a groundbreaking twist: players could invest real money in virtual livestock, which supposedly funded actual farms whose profits would flow back to investors. The hybrid gaming-crowdfunding platform became a sensation, attracting over 500,000 active players and 130,000 real investors who contributed approximately $250 million, with some taking out loans believing they were simultaneously supporting Turkey's struggling agriculture sector. Iden leveraged celebrity endorsements and franchise farm shops throughout the country to build legitimacy and operated a classic Ponzi scheme, paying early investors with money from new recruits while most farms remained inactive fronts. By late 2017, regulatory scrutiny increased, prompting Iden to sell his shares and vanish before the scheme officially collapsed in March 2018. Despite an Interpol red notice, he fled to South America, living lavishly in Uruguay before eventually turning himself in at the Brazilian consulate in 2019. He was extradited to Turkey facing 89,000 years in prison, having stolen $8.5 million while claiming authorities prevented payouts. The case exemplifies how a charismatic founder exploited national economic concerns and the "too good to be true" appeal to defraud hundreds of thousands of ordinary citizens.
