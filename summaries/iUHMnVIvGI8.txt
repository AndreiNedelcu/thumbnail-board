# Summary

This video examines the dot-com bubble of the late 1990s and early 2000s, tracing how excessive speculation and hype created one of history's most dramatic market crashes. Beginning with the 1993 release of Mosaic browser, which made the internet widely accessible, the narrative follows how rapidly improving technology, exploding website growth, and landmark events like Netscape's 1995 IPO triggered a gold-rush mentality. Fueled by low interest rates, favorable capital gains taxes, and venture capital eager to fund growth-at-any-cost startups, hundreds of dot-com companies attracted billions in investment despite lacking viable business models or profitability. The bubble burst in 2000-2002 due to multiple triggers: Alan Greenspan's interest rate hikes, Japan's recession, the Y2K doomsday that never materialized, and Microsoft's antitrust lawsuit. Companies that spent lavishly on advertising rather than revenue collapsed, destroying $1.75 trillion in value and tens of thousands of jobs. The video analyzes responsibility, attributing the disaster primarily to venture capitalists, investment banks, and the Federal Reserve's role in creating excess capital. It concludes by comparing historical bubbles to present-day IPO activity, suggesting cycles repeat when market exuberance overwhelms rational analysis. The target audience is young professionals and entrepreneurs seeking to understand market cycles and investing lessons.
