Japan is constructing the Hyper-Kamiokande, a $600 million neutrino observatory being carved into Mount Ikenoyama in the Japanese Alps, representing the world's largest neutrino detection facility. The project involves excavating a massive cylindrical cavern—88 meters tall and 69 meters wide, equivalent to a Boeing 747 standing upright—located 681 meters beneath the mountain's surface. This extreme depth provides crucial protection from cosmic rays while allowing neutrinos to pass through unimpeded. Once completed in 2026, the cavern will be filled with 260 million liters of ultra-pure water containing 40,000 highly sensitive photon detectors that capture faint Cherenkov radiation produced when neutrinos collide with electrons. The project builds on decades of neutrino research at the site, succeeding the original Kamiokande (1983) and Super-Kamiokande (1996) experiments.
