# Summary

This video examines why public distrust in the official JFK assassination narrative persists despite the Warren Commission's definitive conclusion that Lee Harvey Oswald acted alone. The creator traces how institutional secrecy and political motivations shaped the investigation from the beginning. He explains Kennedy's conflicts with the CIA and military intelligence over covert operations, then details how FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover prematurely concluded Oswald's guilt before investigation began, influencing the hastily assembled Warren Commission—which included Allen Dulles, a CIA director Kennedy had fired. The video scrutinizes the "single-bullet theory" that supposedly caused seven wounds, highlights how the commission suppressed contradicting witness testimony and medical reports, and reveals how government officials prioritized a simplified narrative to prevent Cold War escalation with the Soviet Union and Cuba. The creator argues that withholding information backfired catastrophically: when the Zapruder film aired 12 years later and Americans witnessed eroding government trust through Vietnam and Watergate, public skepticism surged. A 1979 congressional investigation officially concluded conspiracy was probable and criticized the FBI and CIA for withholding evidence, yet stopped short of identifying perpetrators. The video targets viewers interested in American history, institutional accountability, and how government transparency failures fuel conspiracy theories.
