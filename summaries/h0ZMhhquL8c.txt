This video recounts the 1911 theft of the Mona Lisa from the Louvre Museum in Paris, one of history's most famous art heists. The narrative follows the actual crime's execution: an Italian handyman named Vincenzo Peruggia hid in the museum overnight, removed the painting from its frame the next morning, and walked out with it under his arm. The video details the subsequent investigation, which lasted two years and involved numerous false leads, including the arrest of Pablo Picasso as a suspect. Police eventually discovered Peruggia living just two miles from the Louvre with the painting hidden in a trunk; he was caught after sending an anonymous letter to an art dealer in Florence offering to return the work. The video also presents an alternative theory from a 1932 account suggesting the theft was part of an elaborate forgery scheme orchestrated by art forger Eduardo De Valero, who had commissioned Peruggia to steal the original while six copies were sold to wealthy American collectors. The content appeals to audiences interested in art history, true crime, and historical mysteries, blending documented facts with speculative narrative elements.
