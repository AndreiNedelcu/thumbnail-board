This video traces the history of the Tsar Bomba, the most powerful nuclear weapon ever detonated at 50 megatons in 1961. It explains how Soviet leader Nikita Khrushchev, seeking to assert dominance after diplomatic failures and the U-2 spy plane incident, pressured Soviet physicist Andrei Sakharov's team to build a 100-megaton bomb in just 16 weeks. Sakharov, troubled by the humanitarian consequences of nuclear testing and calculating that detonation would cause 660,000 premature deaths from radiation, made a last-minute design change using lead instead of uranium to reduce the yield to 50 megatons. The video details the detonation on October 30, 1961, describing its unprecedented scale—visible from 1,000 kilometers away, creating a mushroom cloud 64 kilometers high, and producing shock waves that circled Earth three times. Though relatively "clean" for a thermonuclear weapon, the test scattered radioactive contamination globally. Sakharov subsequently became a vocal disarmament advocate, helping catalyze the 1963 Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty. The video targets general audiences interested in Cold War history, nuclear weapons technology, and the ethical implications of weapons development, concluding by noting the U.S. had similarly ambitious but ultimately untested megaton projects.
