# Summary

This video documents a controlled garden experiment comparing how six different food scraps affect tomato plant growth when buried directly in soil. The experimenter buried sourdough starter with tahini pasta, garden clippings, catfish heads and fillets, mixed kitchen scraps, farm-fresh eggs, and nothing (control) beneath identically-grown Italian Roma tomatoes under identical conditions including controlled watering via drip irrigation and protective mesh against gophers. Over several months, the results revealed that larger, complex organic matter like whole catfish heads decomposed too slowly to benefit plants effectively, producing stunted growth and minimal fruit. Mid-sized, chopped kitchen scraps performed averagely, while garden weeds produced the most vegetative growth, possibly due to quick nitrogen availability. Most surprisingly, the pure control tomato and the egg-buried tomato produced the most fruit, with the control yielding over 70 ripe tomatoes compared to 30 or fewer from amended plants. Digging up each planting site showed that small, high-surface-area materials decomposed completely within months, while larger items like catfish bones remained visible. The experiment targets gardeners interested in soil amendment techniques, challenging the conventional wisdom that burying food scraps directly benefits plants, and suggesting composting creates more bioavailable nutrients than raw burial.
