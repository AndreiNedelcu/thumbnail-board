Clinical burnout is a specific psychological syndrome distinct from ordinary stress, characterized by three core symptoms: emotional exhaustion that persists even after rest, depersonalization or cynicism toward colleagues and clients, and a sense of personal ineffectiveness despite performing well. A psychologist explains that burnout develops from organizational factors including overwhelming workloads, misalignment between personal values and job demands, perceived unfairness, and loss of intrinsic rewards. The condition has historical roots but was extensively researched by Christina Maslak in the 1980s-90s. To assess whether someone is experiencing burnout rather than temporary stress, individuals should evaluate whether they feel emotionally drained beyond physical tiredness, notice changes in interpersonal relationships marked by reduced patience and compassion, and question whether their work feels meaningful. Treatment requires both organizational changes—adjusting workloads, values alignment, and rewards—and personal strategies including maintaining social connections outside work, preserving hobbies, and preventing excessive identity investment in employment. The video targets working professionals concerned about their mental health and those in helping professions susceptible to burnout.
