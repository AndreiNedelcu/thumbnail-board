This comprehensive review examines Valve's Steam Machine, a compact gaming PC designed for living room play. The device runs SteamOS, a Linux-based operating system featuring Proton for Windows game compatibility and console-like conveniences such as game suspension and resume functionality. A standout feature is HDMI CEC support, enabling seamless control of multiple media devices through a single remote or controller—something most PCs lack. The Steam Machine uses custom Zen 4 CPU hardware with DDR5 RAM and an AMD GPU, targeting performance superior to approximately 70% of existing gaming PCs. However, the review criticizes Valve's "4K at 60 FPS" marketing claims, revealing that newer titles achieve this only through aggressive FSR upscaling from 720p with low settings, resulting in visual compromises. Ray tracing performance is particularly weak, with the GPU struggling significantly. The $1,050–$1,350 pricing is deemed expensive compared to PlayStation 5 alternatives, though the device offers open peripheral compatibility, repairability, and free online multiplayer—advantages over console ecosystems. The review targets PC gamers and early adopters interested in Valve's open-platform approach, noting that while the Steam Machine delivers an excellent living room experience, its performance and premium pricing limit mainstream appeal compared to subsidized console options.
