# Summary

This video chronicles the true story of the MIT Blackjack Team, a group of college students who systematically beat casinos using card counting and coordinated strategies between 1979 and the early 1990s. Led by JP Massar, Bill Kaplan, and others, the team applied mathematical principles and computer simulations to demonstrate that blackjack could be beaten consistently through the high-low card counting method combined with team-based signaling systems. Players were divided into spotters, controllers, and big players who communicated through subtle gestures to place optimal bets when the deck favored them. The team underwent rigorous training in isolated environments and tested their skills at casinos before official deployment. After recruiting over 70 players across a decade, they achieved remarkable returns on investment—sometimes exceeding 300% annually—by operating from Atlantic City and later the newly opened Foxwood Casino in Connecticut. The narrative explores how these students balanced their MIT education with their clandestine gambling operations, maintaining operational security while growing their bankroll from initial seed money to over a million dollars through careful management and mathematical precision.
