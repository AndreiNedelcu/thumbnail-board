The video explores a major evolutionary bottleneck event that occurred 800,000 to 900,000 years ago, when the human ancestral population crashed to approximately 1,280 individuals—wiping out 98.7% of early humans. Originally, scientists attributed a 1970s-discovered genetic bottleneck to the Lake Toba volcanic eruption 74,000 years ago, but modern research has largely debunked this theory. Instead, recent genomic analysis of over 3,000 modern humans revealed an earlier catastrophe during the Calabrian stage. The primary suspect is the Middle Pleistocene Transition, a shift in Earth's glacial cycles, particularly the 0.9 million-year-ago event, which caused extreme cold and arid conditions across Africa and Eurasia. This made hunting difficult and forced early humans to adapt their diets. The lack of fire technology likely exacerbated survival challenges, though populations recovered dramatically once fire mastery spread. The video targets audiences interested in human evolution, paleontology, and natural history, discussing competing hypotheses and ongoing scientific debate about whether climate change, migration patterns, or other factors caused this near-extinction event that shaped modern human genetics.
