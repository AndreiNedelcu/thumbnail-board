Neurocapitalism represents an emerging economic system that merges surveillance capitalism with neuroscience to commodify human cognition, emotions, and attention. The concept, popularized through Giorgio Agamben's work, describes how corporations leverage brain-monitoring technology, biometrics, and data-driven psychology to extract value from neural activity—from measuring student focus in Chinese classrooms to monitoring worker stress on factory floors. Like previous economic systems that evolved to exploit new resources, neurocapitalism targets the mind itself as a marketplace, transforming our thoughts and cognitive processes into exploitable commodities. The system relies on individuals willingly surrendering their neural data through digital interactions, making every scroll, click, and glance a source of extractable cognitive value. A particular concern involves digital conditioning of children, whose developing brains are especially vulnerable to algorithmic manipulation, raising fundamental questions about human autonomy and free will. This framework positions neurocapitalism as the latest frontier of capitalist optimization, moving beyond industrial production and platform-dependent tech feudalism to directly commodify human consciousness and mental potential.
