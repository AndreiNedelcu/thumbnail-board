Roy, founder of Cluey, built Interview Coder, an AI tool that helps users cheat on software engineering technical interviews by providing real-time answers through a screen overlay—a novel interface that got him expelled from Columbia University. Rather than viewing this as a setback, he leveraged the viral attention to pivot toward a larger vision: a desktop AI assistant that helps users "cheat on everything" by seamlessly integrating AI assistance into daily tasks. The company has raised $5.3 million in seed funding and is approaching $5 million in annual recurring revenue. Roy discusses his philosophy on viral growth, emphasizing that controversy and polarization drive engagement on platforms like X/Twitter, while maintaining clear boundaries between his online persona and real life. Beyond the interview-cheating angle, he positions Cluey as pioneering a new form of human-AI interaction through multimodal AI that understands screen context and audio, targeting users who want AI assistance integrated seamlessly into work and meetings. He argues this represents the future of human augmentation and productivity, encouraging young entrepreneurs to take bigger risks in the AI space where domain expertise can be developed quickly regardless of age.
