# Summary

This investigation examines the Smokehouse Creek Fire, Texas's largest wildfire in history, which burned over one million acres in February 2024 and destroyed homes, ranches, and livelihoods across the Panhandle. The fire was ignited by a downed utility pole owned by Xcel Energy, a Minneapolis-based electric and natural gas company, yet executives denied negligence despite evidence that the pole was poorly maintained and marked for replacement. The video documents the catastrophic impact on ranchers like Jason Abraham, who lost cattle, infrastructure, and hundreds of thousands of dollars, while exploring how utility company negligence compounds with climate change and drought to create dangerous wildfire conditions. It reveals systemic problems including inadequate pole inspections (contracted to Osmos), cost-cutting maintenance practices, and Xcel's history of igniting hundreds of fires nationwide. The investigation addresses potential solutions—from holding utilities accountable through massive liability payments to transitioning toward publicly owned utility models—and questions whether rate-payers should bear costs when private companies profit from negligent infrastructure. The video targets audiences concerned with corporate accountability, rural communities affected by utilities, and those interested in the intersection of environmental disaster, regulation, and corporate responsibility.
