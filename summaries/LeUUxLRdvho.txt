# Summary

This conversation between a software engineer and Basam Daidi, a senior engineer at GitHub, explores the critical gap between system design theory and practical implementation in software engineering. The discussion challenges the tech industry's tendency to over-engineer solutions by adopting complex architectures prematurely—such as Kubernetes or NoSQL databases—driven by status-seeking rather than genuine business needs. Drawing from GitHub's experience managing millions of requests per second on minimal infrastructure, Daidi advocates for designing systems incrementally: start simple, wait until reaching 80% resource capacity before scaling, and use vertical scaling before horizontal solutions. He emphasizes that most technology doesn't last beyond a decade, making long-term architectural bets risky, and recommends designing for the next order of magnitude rather than distant futures. The conversation also addresses the business tension between predictable costs and software's inherent need for continuous evolution and maintenance investments. Daidi stresses solving specific, concrete problems over generic solutions, avoiding premature optimization, and regularly revisiting architectural decisions quarterly or annually rather than setting decade-long strategies. The target audience includes mid-level and senior engineers seeking to strengthen system design judgment and CTOs/founders wanting practical guidance on scaling decisions.
