# Summary

This video traces the intellectual foundations of artificial intelligence and computer science through ten pivotal papers spanning nearly a century. It begins with Alan Turing's 1936 work on computable numbers, which established the theoretical limits of algorithms and inadvertently defined the blueprint for all modern computers. Claude Shannon's 1948 mathematical theory of communication introduced information theory and the bit, providing the mathematical framework for measuring uncertainty and prediction—concepts fundamental to modern AI loss functions. The narrative continues through Frank Rosenblatt's perceptron, the MIT paper exposing its limitations, Leslie Lamport's work on distributed systems enabling large-scale computing, and Hinton's backpropagation algorithm that enabled neural networks to learn. The video then covers Google's PageRank algorithm, the 2012 ImageNet breakthrough with AlexNet demonstrating deep learning's viability, and the transformer architecture introduced in "Attention is All You Need," which revolutionized language models. Finally, it examines OpenAI's GPT-3, which proved that scaling neural networks to unprecedented sizes produces emergent intelligence. The video targets audiences interested in AI history, computer science fundamentals, and understanding how contemporary large language models evolved from decades of theoretical and practical advances.
