Australia's housing crisis, particularly acute in Sydney—ranked the second least affordable market globally among 94 major cities—serves as a cautionary tale for the world. The crisis stems from multiple factors: a 33% surge in national home prices since the pandemic, record-low vacancy rates (under one percent), and skyrocketing rents that increased 16-20% annually. Supply constraints are critical, with Sydney needing roughly 900,000 additional dwellings by 2041 to accommodate population growth, while construction firms face bankruptcy due to pandemic-driven inflation raising building costs by 30%. The documentary follows real people like Harry, a student forced into precarious rental situations with 85 applicants competing for single properties, illustrating how younger and lower-income Australians bear the crisis's brunt. Rising interest rates compound the problem for first-time homebuyers, while tight rental markets leave workers like Harry's mother—a teacher unable to afford her longtime neighborhood—displaced. The analysis targets policymakers and global audiences concerned with housing affordability, examining root causes including insufficient social and affordable housing stock, construction industry collapse, and immigration-driven demand, while exploring potential solutions like densification and increased government investment in social housing.
