# Summary

This video explores whether a nuclear submarine could function as a spacecraft in Earth orbit by examining the engineering and physics challenges involved. The analysis reveals that while submarine hulls are structurally robust enough to contain internal pressure and maintain reasonable airtight seals, the primary obstacles stem from life support and thermal management. Submarines generate oxygen from seawater using electricity, which becomes impossible in space, limiting survivability to only a few days on stored oxygen reserves. The critical problem, however, is heat dissipation: a 200-megawatt nuclear reactor designed to dump heat into seawater would cause a catastrophic meltdown within an hour in the vacuum of space, since radiation is an ineffective cooling mechanism. To deorbit, the submarine would need to fire its Trident ballistic missiles backward—a maneuver that would barely provide sufficient velocity change while leaving the vessel uncontrolled and aerodynamically unstable. The submarine would inevitably break apart during atmospheric reentry, though theoretically someone secured in the right position might survive deceleration and potentially escape with a parachute. The video targets audiences interested in speculative engineering, space physics, and thought experiments about applying existing technology beyond its intended purpose.
