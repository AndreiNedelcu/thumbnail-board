# Summary

Organizational psychologist Bob Sutton explores how leaders can strategically reduce or increase friction in organizations to make right actions easier and wrong actions harder. Using the analogy of successful race car teams who know when to brake versus accelerate, Sutton explains that friction isn't inherently bad—it's about applying it purposefully. He illustrates this through contrasting examples: Elizabeth Holmes facing regulatory friction (good) while attempting illegal blood-testing technology versus a tampon company navigating FDA approval (constructive friction that validated their product). Sutton defines a "friction fixer" as someone who acts as a trustee of others' time, making strategic decisions about what should be frictionless and what should be difficult. He emphasizes two diagnostic questions leaders should ask: whether they truly understand what they're doing, and whether a decision is reversible. The DMV employee story and Google Glass example demonstrate how slowing down on irreversible, complex decisions prevents costly mistakes, while reversible decisions should move quickly. This framework targets leaders, managers, and organizational professionals seeking to improve efficiency by aligning friction with organizational values and outcomes.
