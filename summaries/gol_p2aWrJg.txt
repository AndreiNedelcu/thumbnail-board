In 1880s Paris, Austrian engineer Victor Popp created a revolutionary pneumatic clock distribution system that synchronized thousands of clocks across the city using bursts of compressed air through underground pipes. This ingenious solution to timekeeping coordination emerged from the practical need to standardize time as cities modernized beyond hourly bell tower announcements. The system centered on a master clock housed at the Pneumatic Clock Company, which split into two mechanisms: one maintaining precise time via pendulum (updated daily from Paris Observatory astronomical observations) and another controlling when compressed air pulses were released into the network. Each pulse through the distributed pipes reached clocks mounted in homes, train stations, hotels, and public streets, where simple bellows mechanisms advanced gear-driven minute and hour hands by exactly one minute per pulse. The subscriber-based service charged 30 cents daily per clock, making it accessible to both institutions and households. Though damaged during Paris's catastrophic 1910 flood, the system persisted until 1927 when reliable electrical clocks made it obsolete.
