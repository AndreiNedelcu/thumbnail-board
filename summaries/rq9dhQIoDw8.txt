This video examines why Red 3, a synthetic food dye linked to thyroid cancer in rats and behavioral problems in children, remains approved and widely used in approximately 3,000 American food products despite regulatory requirements suggesting it should be banned. The video traces the history of food dye regulation in the US, explaining how the 1958 Food Additives Amendment and the Delaney Clause—which mandates banning additives shown to cause cancer in animals—should have triggered Red 3's removal in 1990 when cancer risks were identified during cosmetics review. However, the FDA has delayed action for over 30 years. The video highlights that Red 3 is already banned in the European Union and cosmetics in the US, yet remains in food, while newer research suggests potential neurobehavioral effects in children beyond cancer risk. California's 2027 ban on Red 3 is expected to create a de facto national prohibition, as major manufacturers already produce safer alternatives for European markets. The video is targeted at consumers interested in food safety, regulatory policy, and public health, emphasizing that these dyes serve purely cosmetic purposes with no nutritional or functional benefit.
