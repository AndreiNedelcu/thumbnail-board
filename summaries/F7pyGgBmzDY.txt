In the Thar Desert of Northwest India, home to 25 million people in extreme conditions, communities have developed ingenious water management systems that transform arid landscapes into productive farmland. The video explores three interconnected techniques that enable survival and agriculture in one of the world's hottest, driest regions. First, strategically placed surface ponds capture monsoon runoff during brief but intense rainfall events, creating permanent water sources that supply both domestic and agricultural needs. Second, individual farm fields function as shallow rainwater collection basins that overflow sequentially during rainstorms, allowing farmers to grow crops using moisture stored in the soil even with minimal annual rainfall. Third, household-scale underground tanks called "tonas" built from ferrocement collect rooftop and ground-surface runoff, providing domestic water security while freeing women and children from long daily water-fetching walks. The video documents the work of Gravis, an organization that has spent 40 years implementing these techniques across the region, demonstrating that water resilience, food resilience, and climate resilience are achievable even in severely marginal environments. The approach integrates diverse tree planting for erosion control, fodder, and firewood while supporting rainfed agriculture and community development.
