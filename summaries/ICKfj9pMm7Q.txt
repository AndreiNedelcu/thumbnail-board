This video explores how metacognition—thinking about your own thinking and emotions—can enhance emotional intelligence and self-regulation. The speaker explains that emotions originate in the limbic system, a non-conscious part of the brain that generates instinctive feelings and drives. However, humans possess a prefrontal cortex capable of consciously processing these emotions and choosing appropriate responses. Metacognition bridges this gap by creating awareness of emotional reactions, allowing individuals to reflect on feelings rather than being controlled by them. The video illustrates this concept using the example of teaching children emotional regulation—encouraging them to "use their words" instead of reacting emotionally. Practical techniques for metacognitive self-management are discussed, including the classic advice to count to ten (or ideally thirty) before responding to anger, giving the prefrontal cortex time to regulate the limbic system's impulses. The speaker emphasizes that metacognition requires consistent practice and self-compassion, noting that developing these habits improves both personal happiness and one's positive impact on others. Research shows that people who successfully moderate their emotions rather than react impulsively experience greater life satisfaction and interpersonal harmony.
