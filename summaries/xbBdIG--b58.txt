# Summary

This documentary explores Africa's Great Green Wall, a massive land restoration initiative across the Sahel region targeting desertification and food insecurity. Working with the United Nations World Food Program, the filmmaker documents how half a million people have been lifted from food aid dependency through ecological regeneration projects in Niger. The core restoration technique involves "half moons"—simple water-harvesting structures that capture rainfall, prevent soil erosion, and enable tree and vegetation growth in drought-prone areas. Over ten years, the initiative has restored approximately 300,000 hectares, with ripple effects benefiting three times that area through improved water infiltration, windbreaks, temperature regulation, and biodiversity enhancement. The restored landscapes provide year-round food security through livestock grazing, harvested vegetation, and elevated groundwater tables that enable market gardening. The project demonstrates how addressing underlying environmental degradation—rather than temporary food aid—can sustainably resolve regional hunger. While currently addressing only a fraction of the Sahel's needs, the scalable approach represents a transformative solution matching the problem's magnitude, combining community participation, government commitment, and international support to rebuild ecological and food security systems.
