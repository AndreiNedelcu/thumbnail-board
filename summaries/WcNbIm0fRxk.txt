Casper, once a celebrated direct-to-consumer mattress startup, serves as a cautionary tale about the limitations of the D2C business model that captivated venture capitalists in the 2010s. The video examines how Casper raised $340 million with a pre-IPO valuation of $1.1 billion by leveraging aggressive advertising on Facebook, Instagram, and podcasts to eliminate traditional retail middlemen and offer cheaper products directly to consumers. However, the company's public collapse reveals fundamental economic flaws: it lost money every year of operation despite 50% gross margins because it spent 60-73% of profits on advertising, which became increasingly expensive as platforms saturated and competition intensified. By 2020, when Facebook ad costs surged 93%, Casper's growth slumped to just 18%, making profitability impossible. The video demonstrates that mattress sales—a low-frequency, long-cycle product—cannot sustain D2C profitability without venture capital subsidies, and that Casper ironically needed to open physical retail stores to survive, ironically validating the traditional model it sought to disrupt. This analysis targets business students, entrepreneurs, and investors interested in understanding why thousands of D2C startups have failed despite initial hype.
