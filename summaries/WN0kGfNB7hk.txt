This video explores the history and future of human artifacts left in space, particularly focusing on satellites in geostationary orbit as potential final evidence of human civilization. It begins with the Apollo 11 moon landing and its connection to a 30,000-year-old cave discovered in Namibia, asking what humanity's space artifacts will mean millennia from now. The creator examines different orbital zones—low Earth orbit, medium Earth orbit, and geostationary orbit—explaining how altitude affects how long objects remain in space. Geostationary satellites, parked 35,786 kilometers above the equator, will persist for approximately 5 billion years, possibly outlasting human civilization itself. The video delves into the geopolitical history of satellite allocation, beginning with Sputnik in 1957 and the subsequent space race, detailing how the United Nations established space as common heritage for all nations while developed countries and corporations monopolized the most valuable orbital real estate. It discusses early equatorial nations' failed Bogotá Declaration attempting to claim sections of geostationary orbit, setting up tensions between wealthy spacefaring nations and developing countries excluded from space infrastructure benefits. The video targets audiences interested in space history, technology, international law, and the long-term consequences of human exploration.
