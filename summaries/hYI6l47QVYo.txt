# Summary

Apple has executed a decade-long strategy to transform from a hardware manufacturer into a comprehensive financial services provider, leveraging its dominant iPhone ecosystem and the growing fintech market valued at over $294 billion. Beginning with the Wallet app's evolution from a simple ticket-holder to a financial hub, Apple has systematically introduced contactless payments through Apple Pay (2014), peer-to-peer transfers via Apple Cash (2017), credit services through the Apple Card partnership with Goldman Sachs (2019), buy-now-pay-later options with Apple Pay Later (2023), and savings accounts through Apple Savings (2024). This strategy serves dual purposes: generating recurring revenue as hardware sales decline, and deepening customer lock-in by intertwining financial services with iPhone usage, making brand switching economically painful. Apple partners with established financial institutions to avoid obtaining banking licenses and regulatory compliance burdens. However, partnerships have proven contentious—Goldman Sachs has reportedly lost billions on the Apple Card and sought exit negotiations—prompting Apple to explore in-house capabilities through Project Breakout, though its Pay Later shutdown suggests challenges remain. The initiative has not gone unnoticed by legacy financial institutions, who view Apple Wallet as an existential threat and are developing competing solutions like Pay to reclaim market share and weaken Apple's ecosystem dominance.
