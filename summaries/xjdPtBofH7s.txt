# Summary

This video explores the decades-long mystery of Apollo 10's lunar module "Snoopy," which was jettisoned during the 1969 moon mission and lost to human observation for over fifty years. After the crew descended to within 15 kilometers of the lunar surface as a rehearsal for Apollo 11, they separated the ascent stage and performed a burn to send it away from Earth and the moon. Using orbital mechanics and positional data from the Earth, Moon, and Sun, the video explains how Snoopy's trajectory changed over time—initially entering an orbit faster than Earth's, then slowing relative to the sun due to gravity, and eventually completing laps around the sun every 342 days. In 2018, astronomers identified a faint object matching predictions for Snoopy's location, exhibiting orbital characteristics and reflectivity properties consistent with the spacecraft. The video targets space enthusiasts and Apollo history buffs, explaining complex orbital physics accessibly while detailing the detective work involved in locating this lost artifact of human spaceflight. Definitive confirmation awaits a predicted 2028 close approach when spectroscopy observations could finally verify the object's composition.
