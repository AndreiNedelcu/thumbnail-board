China's population declined for the first time in six decades in 2022, marking a critical turning point with profound economic and social implications. The video traces how historical policies shaped this crisis: Mao's devastating famine killed 30 million people, followed by a baby boom that alarmed the government into implementing the "later, longer, fewer" campaign and, later, the strict one-child policy from 1980, which relied on forced sterilizations and induced abortions to suppress growth. Though effective at controlling population, these measures created a unique 4-to-1 family structure where only children face immense pressure caring for aging parents, discouraging larger families despite recent policy reversals. China's fertility rate has remained well below the replacement rate of 2.1 for over three decades, and financial pressures continue preventing young people from having multiple children. The video explains how China leveraged its massive population to become a manufacturing superpower and the world's second-largest economy, but rapid aging and a shrinking workforce now threaten both its economic future and role globally. Unlike other developed nations experiencing gradual aging, China's demographic crisis happened remarkably fast—only forty years after exploiting population growth for economic dominance, the country now faces unprecedented challenges in supporting its elderly population and maintaining economic momentum.
