This video explores what constitutes the rarest move in chess by analyzing algebraic notation and statistical data from millions of games. The creator examines standard chess notation conventions, including how moves are written (e.g., E4, Nxf3) and how disambiguation works when multiple pieces can move to the same square. After analyzing all 342 billion moves from Lichess's database of 5 billion games, the video identifies four rare move categories: doubly disambiguated queen capture checkmates, rank-disambiguated bishop capture checkmates, doubly disambiguated bishop checkmates via discovered attack, and doubly disambiguated knight capture checkmates. Most of these require pawn underpromotion to create multiple pieces of the same type. Through detailed statistical analysis, the creator finds that the rarest moves occur fewer than 200 times in the entire dataset, with some never occurring at all. The video targets chess enthusiasts interested in both gameplay mechanics and statistical analysis, combining educational content about notation with surprising empirical findings about chess rarity.
