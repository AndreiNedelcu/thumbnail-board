# Summary

Modulex was a lesser-known LEGO product line created in the 1960s by Godtfred Kirk Christensen, the company founder's son, to address a fundamental problem with standard LEGO bricks: their 5x5x6 proportional ratio made designing realistically scaled buildings nearly impossible. Modulex bricks featured a revolutionary 1x1x1 cubic ratio and measured exactly 5mm per side, perfectly matching the 1:20 architectural scale standard in Europe, allowing professional architects to translate models directly into real-world blueprints. The line was intentionally separated from LEGO's consumer toy brand through distinctive muted colors (olive green, terracotta, teal blue, buff, ochre), smaller brick dimensions, and removed LEGO branding to appeal to serious design professionals. Though initially successful in architecture, modulex pivoted toward industrial design, factory layout planning, campus management, and the highly successful modulex Planning System—which used printed tiles to help businesses across multiple countries including NASA, Volkswagen, and Ford manage workflows and track projects. Despite its professional applications and genuine innovation, modulex gradually declined as computer-aided design emerged and building styles shifted toward modern glass-and-steel structures incompatible with the system's classical design focus. The product line represents an innovative but ultimately unsuccessful attempt to establish LEGO as a serious industrial tool rather than a children's toy.
