# Summary

This video explores why major mapping applications like Google Maps and Apple Maps don't display unsafe areas or crime hotspots despite having access to vast amounts of data, using a thought experiment about building such a feature from scratch. The creator walks through iterative product versions, starting with a simple overlay marking dangerous neighborhoods based on police crime statistics, which fails because high-population city centers appear disproportionately dangerous. A second approach introduces crowdsourcing similar to Waze's hazard reporting, but this generates significant ethical backlash as business owners and residents of flagged areas face discrimination, reduced tourism, and plummeting property values. Subsequent versions attempt to hide safety scores from users while using AI to validate crowdsourced reports and identify genuine threats, but this introduces new problems including data manipulation, spam, and questions about private companies controlling critical public safety information. The video demonstrates how what appears to be an obvious feature involves profound complexities around fairness, discrimination, public anxiety, business impact, and governmental authority—explaining why major tech companies have largely opted not to implement such functionality despite having the technical capability.
