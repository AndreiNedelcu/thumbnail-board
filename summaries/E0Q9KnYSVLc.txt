# Summary

This video traces Linux's evolution from Linus Torvalds's 1991 hobby project to a globally dominant operating system. It contextualizes Linux within earlier computing history, including Unix (1969), the GNU project (1983), and Minix (1987), explaining how Torvalds created the Linux kernel as a free, open-source alternative when existing systems proved inadequate for his Intel 386 microprocessor. The video clarifies the GNU/Linux distinction, noting that while "Linux" technically refers only to the kernel, modern Linux distributions combine GNU tools and components. It covers the kernel's initial public release (September 1991), the pivotal decision to adopt the GNU General Public License (December 1992), and subsequent milestone distributions like Red Hat, Debian, and Ubuntu. The video emphasizes Linux's remarkable growth through collaborative open-source development, documenting its current dominance: powering all top 500 supercomputers, 85% of smartphones, 96% of major web servers, and 90% of cloud infrastructure. It targets computer science enthusiasts and history-minded tech audiences interested in understanding how open-source principles and individual initiative transformed computing infrastructure globally.
