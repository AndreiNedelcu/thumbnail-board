# Summary

The video traces Reddit's dramatic financial trajectory from a $10 billion valuation in 2021 to approximately $5.5 billion two years later, examining the platform's rise and recent controversies. Founded in 2005 by Steve Huffman and Alexis Ohanian, Reddit grew into a dominant social media platform by hosting 138,000+ user-generated communities (subreddits) centered on diverse topics. The platform successfully monetized through advertising strategies and attracted major investment, reaching unicorn status. However, the company faced escalating controversies including the Boston Marathon bombing witch hunt, non-consensual celebrity nude leaks, shared hacked content, and increasingly divisive political content requiring content moderation policies. The pivotal moment came in April 2023 when Reddit announced API charges for developers, drastically changing a seven-year-old free model. This sparked a massive June 2023 blackout involving 8,500 subreddits and 2.5 billion users protesting the decision. The policy particularly impacted popular third-party apps like Apollo, which would face $20 million annual API fees. While some communities migrated to alternatives like Lemmy, most users returned, suggesting Reddit's dominance may persist despite user dissatisfaction, mirroring broader internet trends where platforms retain users despite controversial decisions. The video targets audiences interested in tech industry analysis, platform politics, and digital culture dynamics.
