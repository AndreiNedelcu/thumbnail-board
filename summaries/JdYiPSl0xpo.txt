# Summary

The theatrical window—the exclusive period when movies play only in cinemas before being released elsewhere—has dramatically shrunk over decades due to competition from home viewing technologies and ongoing negotiations between studios and theater chains. Originally stretching nearly a year for films like Toy Story, the window compressed to roughly 74 days pre-pandemic as studios sought to capitalize on multiple revenue streams including digital sales, streaming, and pay-TV. The power dynamics shifted significantly during the COVID-19 pandemic when Universal released Trolls World Tour simultaneously on demand and in empty theaters, generating unprecedented digital revenue. This prompted major studios to shift their focus toward streaming platforms, threatening the theatrical model's viability. However, post-pandemic negotiations resulted in new compromise windows: Disney, Paramount, and Warner Brothers accepted 45-day exclusivity periods, while Universal negotiated the shortest windows ever at 31 and 17 days respectively—but notably without pursuing full same-day releases. Studios ultimately preserved theatrical windows because cinemas remain profitable and offer cultural cachet that streaming cannot replicate, suggesting theaters survived the pandemic stronger than anticipated despite their precarious position.
