# Summary

This video explores creating "Glish," a monosyllabic version of English designed to reduce speaking time by 30 percent. The creator begins by explaining what syllables actually are—not just a counting mechanism, but structures based on vowel sounds and the sonority sequencing principle, which describes how consonant sounds build toward and away from vowels in a natural rhythm. After establishing that syllables form predictable patterns following acoustic loudness gradations, the creator develops a systematic approach to convert multi-syllable English words into single syllables while maintaining pronounceability. The strategy involves prioritizing frequent words, first attempting to extract existing syllables from original words, then generating plausible new syllables using a directed graph that maps which sounds naturally follow others in English. The resulting Glish language successfully compresses many words into monosyllabic forms like "pre" for paragraph and "sibs" for syllables, achieving the intended 30 percent reduction in total syllables per text. However, the new language sacrifices intelligibility—individual mappings seem reasonable but lack obvious meaning to English speakers, making Glish essentially a sister language requiring memorization rather than a transparent shorthand. The creator provides an online translator tool for testing the system.
