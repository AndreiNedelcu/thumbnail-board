# Summary

On November 7, 2007, Nationwide Airlines Flight 723, a Boeing 737-200 departing Cape Town for Johannesburg, experienced a catastrophic engine failure during takeoff that defied pilot training and experience. Captain Trevor Arnold (13,860 hours) and First Officer Daniel Perry (1,000 hours) executed an emergency procedure after the right engine's aft mounting bolt, which had developed a fatigue crack, suddenly fractured during rotation, causing both the secondary support and engine mount to fail. The engine violently swung forward on the forward mount, tearing hydraulic and fuel lines before separating completely from the wing, leaving a gaping hole. Despite this unprecedented scenario—unlike any simulator training—the pilots maintained control through quick reaction inputs and disciplined troubleshooting, following memory items to secure the failed engine while climbing on single-engine power. The incident exposed serious maintenance failures at Nationwide's maintenance organization, which lacked proper licensing and had skipped required engine mount inspections for five years—a critical oversight given the FAA's strict airworthiness directives on the older engine mount bolts prone to metal fatigue. The video is directed at aviation professionals and enthusiasts interested in accident investigation and cockpit decision-making.
