# Summary

This video analyzes the engineering failures of OceanGate's Titan submersible that imploded during a 2023 dive to the Titanic wreck, killing all five occupants aboard. The presenter, a composite materials engineer, explains how the submersible's carbon fiber hull was fundamentally unsuitable for deep-sea applications. While carbon fiber excels in tension, submarine hulls experience compressive forces at depth, making the material vulnerable to snap-buckling and delamination failure modes—issues well-documented in composite engineering literature. OceanGate deliberately avoided industry standard classifications and regulatory oversight, relying instead on computer modeling and a single test dive with inadequate fatigue testing. The composite hull was cured using a cheaper bagging method rather than an autoclave, leaving concerning void content levels. The video documents how the company dismissed expert warnings about internal cracking and delamination, instead installing an acoustic monitoring system incapable of detecting catastrophic failure until it was too late. The presenter argues this represents a dangerous "move fast and break things" mentality in engineering where negligence directly risks human lives, contrasting with properly regulated industries like aviation.
