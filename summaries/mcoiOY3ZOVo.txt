This video explores the surprising history behind why many cheeses are yellow or orange, tracing a narrative that involves medieval farming practices, marketing innovation, and international trade. The core explanation centers on beta carotene, a natural orange pigment found in grass that passes into cow's milk and concentrates in cheese, giving it a yellow hue—whereas sheep cheese remains naturally white since their metabolism doesn't transfer beta carotene to milk. The turning point came in the 1600s when Dutch traders discovered and exported annatto seeds from South America, a cheap, vibrant colorant that replaced expensive alternatives like saffron. Cheese makers began deliberately dyeing their cheese to maintain the appearance of being made from rich, grass-fed milk, establishing a tradition that persists today even though modern factory-farmed cows eat corn and soy rather than grass. The video targets viewers interested in food history and consumer deception, explaining how centuries of marketing created our current expectation for golden cheddar, while noting that naturally colored grass-fed cheese and white undyed varieties remain available alternatives for discerning consumers.
