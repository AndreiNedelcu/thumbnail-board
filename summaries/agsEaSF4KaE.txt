# ADHD and Internalized Shame

This video explores why people with ADHD frequently develop a belief that they are fundamentally broken or defective, despite often having normal or above-average intelligence. The core argument is that when children with undiagnosed or newly diagnosed ADHD recognize they're as intelligent as peers but perform significantly worse academically, they develop a false mathematical conclusion: high effort + poor results = personal failure. This belief is reinforced by authority figures attributing poor performance to lack of effort rather than neurological differences. The presenter explains how this creates learned helplessness and distorted thinking patterns, comparing it to being taught that "two plus two equals five"—where the person internalizes impossible standards. The video discusses the connection between ADHD and depression (70% comorbidity risk if diagnosed with ADHD in childhood), underdiagnosis in girls versus boys, and how medication effectiveness varies by gender since stimulants were developed primarily to address hyperactivity, which manifests more in boys. The content targets people with ADHD, parents, educators, and mental health professionals seeking to understand the psychological mechanisms linking ADHD to depression and shame.
