# Summary

Researchers at the University of Texas at Austin discovered a mysterious pattern of GPS signal disruptions affecting receivers across Europe between 2019 and 2024. Professor Todd Humphre and student Zack Clemens identified 75 days where the signal-to-noise ratio suddenly dropped by a factor of ten across continental distances—from Spain to Poland to Canada—ruling out ground-based interference due to Earth's curvature. The geometric analysis indicated the source had to be at least 1,200 kilometers above Earth, likely a satellite. The interference exhibited unusual characteristics: narrow frequency band (just 5 MHz around 1577.5 MHz), brief 3-5 second bursts, and clustering on weekday business hours, suggesting intentional rather than accidental transmission. The video explains how GPS operates through satellite positioning, atomic clocks, relativistic corrections, and atmospheric compensation before exploring the hunt to identify the culprit from approximately 14 geostationary satellites that could have been visible to all affected stations simultaneously. Initial suspects, including an Algerian communications satellite, proved to be interference victims rather than perpetrators, leaving the mystery largely unresolved. The investigation targets audiences interested in GPS technology, space-based communications, geopolitics, and scientific problem-solving.
