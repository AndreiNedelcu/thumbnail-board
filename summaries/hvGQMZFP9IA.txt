This video examines the critical period from the 1970s through the 1980s when climate science became well-established but policy action stalled, arguing that this decade represented a crucial moment lost in addressing global warming. The narrative traces how scientists like James Hansen, building on earlier work by Roger Revelle and Charles Keeling, developed sophisticated computer models proving that rising CO2 from fossil fuels caused planetary warming—models that were presented to President Carter's administration and achieved scientific consensus. However, the subsequent Reagan administration actively suppressed climate action, utilizing the 1983 "Changing Climate" report to dismiss concerns by emphasizing economic arguments that future climate impacts were too distant to warrant present intervention. The video details how economists like William Nordhaus and Thomas Schelling used discount rates to argue adaptation was cheaper than prevention, creating political cover for inaction. A turning point arrived with the 1985 discovery of the ozone hole over Antarctica, which mobilized rapid international response through the 1987 Montreal Protocol—demonstrating that coordinated global environmental action was possible. This success reinvigorated climate scientists' advocacy for a comparable carbon treaty, suggesting an opportunity was finally emerging. The video targets educated audiences interested in science history, policy analysis, and understanding how institutional and economic forces shaped the climate crisis.
