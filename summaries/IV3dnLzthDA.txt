# Summary

This video explores how Thomas Midgley Jr. and his subsequent impact on public health, orchestrated by decisions made in pursuit of profit, created three major crises affecting millions globally. Midgley, a chemist hired by Kettering to solve engine knocking in high-compression car engines, discovered tetraethyl lead as an additive in 1921—a cheap, effective solution that worked at tiny concentrations. Despite knowing lead's toxicity firsthand after suffering lead poisoning, Midgley promoted it widely, and leaded gasoline became ubiquitous by the 1950s. Chemist Clair Patterson's investigation into lead contamination in his radiometric dating experiments revealed the catastrophic scope: modern Americans carried 1,000 times more lead in their bones than ancient humans, causing permanent neurological damage, reduced IQ by 800 million points collectively, and correlating strongly with 20th-century crime rates. Patterson's research documented lead's devastation through ice cores, ocean samples, and teeth analysis, eventually leading to leaded gasoline bans starting in 1986. Separately, Midgley invented CFCs as refrigerants in 1928—another triumph he demonstrated by inhaling—which later caused the ozone hole. Lead remains responsible for hundreds of thousands of annual deaths from cardiovascular disease, making Midgley's inventions among history's deadliest.
