# Summary

This documentary explores Santa Cruz del Islote, the world's most densely populated island located off the coast of Colombia, where approximately 816 people live in just 0.01 square kilometers—a density of 68,000 people per square kilometer, far exceeding major cities like Hong Kong. The island, artificially enlarged from coral rocks and waste over two centuries, consists of concrete structures, four narrow streets, and homes where ten or more people share single rooms without electricity or running water. The documentary captures the residents' daily struggles, including inadequate housing, limited sanitation and sewage systems, water scarcity dependent on irregular rainfall, and significant ocean pollution from garbage disposal. Despite these hardships, the community maintains strong social bonds with no formal law enforcement, relying instead on elders to resolve disputes. Tourism and fishing provide primary income, while the island has one school, church, clinic, and market. The filmmaker interviews residents about their lives, family planning practices, cultural traditions, and aspirations, ultimately revealing a paradox: while facing severe environmental and infrastructural challenges, the islanders express contentment and strong attachment to their homeland, prioritizing communal happiness over material improvement. The documentary targets audiences interested in anthropology, extreme living conditions, and sociological studies.
