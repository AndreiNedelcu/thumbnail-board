# Summary

This video explores the parallel histories of Adidas and Puma, two global sporting giants founded by feuding brothers Adolf and Rudolf Dassler in post-World War I Germany. Starting from humble origins in a small shoemaking town, the brothers initially collaborated successfully, creating innovative spike shoes and expanding through bold marketing, including sponsoring Jesse Owens at the 1936 Berlin Olympics despite Nazi opposition. However, tensions erupted during World War II when both brothers joined the Nazi party and the company manufactured military equipment. After the war, mutual accusations of betrayal—including alleged denunciations to authorities—shattered their relationship irreparably. In 1948, they split their company and established rival brands on opposite sides of their hometown's river, creating a decades-long competitive feud that extended to their sons and divided their entire community into opposing camps. The rivalry intensified through sponsorship wars and Olympic competitions, with incidents like Puma's secret deal with Pelé at the 1970 World Cup. The brothers died years apart, never reconciling, and were buried at opposite ends of the town cemetery, symbolizing a family rift that remains legendary in business history.
