This video explores what would happen to artificial light sources if humanity suddenly vanished from Earth. It examines various types of lighting infrastructure and their longevity without human maintenance. Power grids would collapse within hours as fossil fuel supply chains fail and nuclear reactors automatically shut down for safety. However, several isolated light sources would persist much longer: diesel generators on remote islands might run for months, geothermal and wind turbines could operate for years before mechanical failures, hydroelectric dams would function for several years, and battery-powered lights would last decades through self-discharge. Solar-powered emergency lights in remote locations represent a strong candidate for the longest-lasting conventional light source, potentially functioning for a century. The surprising answer involves radioactive waste: Cesium-137 mixed into glass blocks produces Cherenkov radiation, creating a distinctive blue glow that would continue for centuries as the material decays. The video combines scientific explanation with practical details about infrastructure maintenance, appealing to audiences interested in physics, apocalyptic scenarios, and how technological systems depend on human intervention.
