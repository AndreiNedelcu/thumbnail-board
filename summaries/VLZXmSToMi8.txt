India faces a rapidly escalating obesity crisis driven primarily by the aggressive marketing and consumption of ultra-processed foods and sugary beverages. Bariatric surgeons like Dr. Ter Mittel report surging patient numbers—from one or two monthly cases to fifteen to twenty—as obesity rates among Indian adults approach one in four, with projections showing an eighty percent increase by 2035. The underlying cause stems from economic and social forces: globalization has transformed dietary habits, with consumption of breakfast cereals, potato chips, and sodas tripling or doubling over recent decades, while multinational corporations like Nestlé and PepsiCo have grown snack food sales from $6.2 billion in 2009 to $32 billion in 2022. This "double burden of malnutrition" creates a paradoxical public health crisis where India simultaneously grapples with childhood stunting and adult obesity-linked diseases including diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and cancer, with estimated economic costs reaching $129 billion by 2035. The documentary examines regulatory responses—comparing India's voluntary company self-regulation against stricter international models like Chile's advertising restrictions—while highlighting grassroots education efforts and the tension between government's desire for foreign investment and health experts' calls for stronger controls on the packaged food industry.
