# Summary

This video traces the scientific history of climate change from its origins in the early 19th century through the mid-20th century. Beginning with a 1912 Popular Mechanics article describing warming weather and crop failures caused by human carbon dioxide emissions, the narrative reveals how climate science developed across centuries through figures like Jean-Baptiste Fourier, who theorized atmospheric insulation in the 1820s, and Eunice Newton Foote, who demonstrated in 1856 that carbon dioxide traps heat—work later credited to John Tyndall. Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius calculated in the 1890s that doubling atmospheric CO2 would warm Earth by 5-6 degrees Celsius, while Guy Callender revisited these ideas in the 1930s. The breakthrough came through oceanographer Roger Revel's discovery that ocean buffering couldn't absorb all human emissions, and Charles David Keeling's precise measurements starting in 1958, which revealed the atmospheric CO2 concentration was steadily rising—the famous Keeling Curve. The video targets audiences interested in scientific history and how our understanding of climate science evolved despite initial scientific skepticism and institutional dismissal.
