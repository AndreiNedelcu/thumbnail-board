The rise of streaming services over the past 15 years fundamentally disrupted the television industry's traditional employment model for writers, ultimately triggering the 2023 writers' strike. While streaming initially seemed promising—expanding the market from 80 to 500+ shows annually and introducing diverse voices to Hollywood—it simultaneously gutted the financial stability that sustained writing careers. Network television offered 40 weeks of guaranteed annual work with steady episode-based pay and robust residuals, allowing writers to build stable lives. Streaming platforms shortened this to 8-10 weeks per season while paying drastically reduced residuals: a single annual check of $400 versus network reruns generating $24,000 per episode. The separation of writers from production phases, shorter episode orders, and reduced job security forced writers to juggle multiple jobs to maintain income, while less experienced writers faced barriers to career development. The strike addresses both economic inequality and creative concerns, with writers arguing that reduced resources and time undermine storytelling quality and exclude economically vulnerable voices from the industry. The conflict reflects broader labor discontent about wage gaps between executives and workers while emphasizing that sustainable, well-resourced creative work benefits both artists and audiences.
