# Summary

This video essay explores how romantic comedies shaped millennial women's unrealistic expectations about love, careers, and lifestyle, examining both the genre's cultural dominance and its decline. The creator analyzes the "golden age" of romcoms (early 1990s to early 2000s) when films like *Legally Blonde*, *The Devil Wears Prada*, and *My Big Fat Greek Wedding* presented protagonists who had glamorous jobs, Manhattan apartments, active social lives, and effortless beauty while seeking their perfect match. The video connects the genre's collapse after 2011 to the rise of mid-budget film disinvestment, the Marvel Cinematic Universe's spectacle-driven dominance, and the decline of monolithic pop culture. It also examines how the 2008 recession created a particularly damaging mismatch for millennials: they came of age during romcom saturation but entered an economy of job scarcity, stagnant wages, and housing unaffordability—the opposite of their screen idols' circumstances. Additionally, the essay critiques the genre's historical racial segregation, noting how mainstream romcoms centered white love stories while parallel Black romcoms rarely crossed into mainstream audiences, ultimately teaching young women of color that romance was primarily for white people. The video targets viewers interested in media criticism, pop culture analysis, and feminist perspectives on entertainment's cultural impact.
