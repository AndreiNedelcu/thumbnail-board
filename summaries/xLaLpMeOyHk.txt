# Summary

This video explores the remarkable water management system of the Alhambra Palace in medieval Granada, Spain, examining how 14th-century engineers solved complex hydraulic challenges without modern technology. The palace, built on a hill 200 meters above the nearest river, required an ingenious multi-stage water system: engineers dammed the river six kilometers upstream and created the Royal Canal to deliver water with precise slope calculations, then used a water wheel-powered well to pressurize the system and fill an elevated storage pool. The video explains how this pressurized water network powered sophisticated features including the famous lion fountain that told time through carefully calibrated holes that activated sequentially, underfloor heating and steam rooms in the thermal baths, and decorative fountains throughout courtyards. Notably, the system incorporated an air-lift pump—a device requiring no external power that used whirlpool-generated pressure to raise water six meters higher. The video demonstrates how evaporation from these water features also served to cool the palace in Granada's hot climate, and how the entire system remained functional for centuries. The content targets audiences interested in medieval engineering, architecture, and innovative problem-solving in historical contexts.
