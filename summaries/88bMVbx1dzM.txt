# Summary

This video explores the history and physics of electron microscopy, from theoretical foundations to modern atomic imaging. The content explains why visible light cannot resolve atoms (due to wavelength limitations) and how physicist Louis de Broglie's 1924 discovery that electrons possess wave properties led to a revolutionary solution. The narrative follows Ernst Ruska's development of the first electron microscope using electromagnetic lenses in the 1930s, then details the spherical aberration problem identified by Otto Scherzer—a fundamental flaw in magnetic lenses that limited resolution for decades. The video traces failed attempts to overcome this barrier through the mid-20th century before highlighting the breakthrough by Nion Urban, Max Haider, and Harald Rose in 1997, who used asymmetric hexapole and octopole magnets to correct aberrations and achieve atomic-level resolution of just 0.13 nanometers. The presentation combines historical narrative with hands-on demonstration at the University of Sydney, targeting scientifically curious audiences interested in how cutting-edge microscopy technology evolved and why it fundamentally transformed materials science and nanotechnology research.
