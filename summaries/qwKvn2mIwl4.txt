Michael Burry, the investor famous for profiting from the 2008 financial crisis, has sold over $70 million in US stock holdings while simultaneously shifting capital into Chinese tech companies, signaling deep concerns about American market valuations. His portfolio moves, tracked through mandatory 13F SEC filings, reveal a strategic retreat from overvalued US equities—particularly tech giants like Amazon and Alphabet—at a time when these stocks have been among the market's strongest performers. Burry's pessimism stems from two primary concerns: the S&P 500's price-to-earnings ratio has reached levels last seen during the late-1990s dot-com bubble, and market concentration has become severely skewed, with just four companies comprising 25% of the index. In contrast, he's increased positions in Alibaba, JD.com, and Baidu, capitalizing on China's historically low valuations (10.2 PE ratio) and substantial government stimulus packages designed to boost consumer spending and economic growth. However, Burry has hedged these Chinese investments with put options worth $43.6 million, protecting against near-term downside risks from US-China trade tensions under Trump's presidency. This dual strategy—abandoning expensive US markets while betting on undervalued Chinese tech with insurance-like hedging—appeals to investors following contrarian, risk-conscious trading approaches.
