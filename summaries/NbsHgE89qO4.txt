# Summary

This video explores the remarkable physics and navigation systems that enabled Voyager 2 to complete its unprecedented 12-year grand tour of the outer solar system beginning in 1977. The spacecraft took advantage of a rare planetary alignment occurring only once every 175 years to visit Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune using gravity assists—a technique where a spacecraft gains speed by passing near a planet's gravitational field. The video details how Voyager 2 achieved this feat despite significant constraints: it had to thread a needle-like 150-kilometer corridor near Jupiter while traveling at 10 kilometers per second, arriving within 1.4 seconds of the target time. The presentation explains Voyager's sophisticated onboard systems, including sun sensors and star trackers for orientation, thrusters for course correction, and gyroscopes for stability. NASA tracked the probe's position and velocity using radio signals traveling at light speed, continuously adjusting its trajectory to ensure successful gravity assists at each planet. The video targets space enthusiasts and those interested in aerospace engineering, celestial mechanics, and the history of space exploration, demonstrating how clever physics and precision engineering enabled one of humanity's greatest exploratory achievements.
