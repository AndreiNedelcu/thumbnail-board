This video explores the philosophy and science of color by examining how humans perceive, interpret, and conceptualize colors across biological, cultural, and philosophical dimensions. The presentation traces the distinction between Newton's scientific approach—treating color as electromagnetic wavelengths detected by cone cells in the retina—and Goethe's phenomenological approach, which emphasizes color as a lived perceptual experience shaped by context and human interaction. The video discusses cross-modal correspondence (synesthesia, the bouba-kiki effect), Berlin and Kay's linguistic study of color categories across cultures, and psychological associations between colors and concepts. It examines optical illusions demonstrating that color perception depends on context rather than purely physical properties, then positions two philosophical frameworks: Locke's primary-secondary quality distinction (treating color as a dispositional property of objects) and the ecological action-based theory (viewing color as a relational property emerging from organism-environment interaction). The video emphasizes that color varies across species based on evolutionary adaptation, using examples like deep-sea fish monochromacy and primate evolution alongside fruiting plants. This content targets viewers interested in philosophy of perception, cognitive science, and how human experience bridges physical reality and conscious awareness.
