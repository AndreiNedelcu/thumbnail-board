# Summary

After reading over 100 philosophy books, the speaker identifies works that fundamentally shaped their worldview and approach to life. *Ishmael* challenges the destructive myth that nature exists to be conquered, proposing instead a harmonious human-nature relationship. Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* introduces virtue ethics and the "golden mean"—finding balance between extremes through reasoned action—applicable to daily decisions. Buddhist teachings explore suffering, non-self, and attachment, while Zen Buddhism delves into transcending conceptual thinking to grasp the mind's true nature. Simone de Beauvoir's *The Ethics of Ambiguity* establishes moral obligations to recognize others' freedom rather than treating them as fixed objects, supporting feminist philosophy. Ichiro Kishimi's adaptation of Adlerian psychology emphasizes present choices over past determination and the courage to be disliked. Dostoevsky's *Crime and Punishment* examines moral rationalization through a protagonist tormented by murder. Camus's *The Stranger* illustrates the absurd—humanity's futile search for meaning in an indifferent world. Nietzsche's *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* presents the Übermensch concept, advocating self-affirmation beyond religious morality. Asimov's *Foundation* explores psychohistory's ethical implications regarding population manipulation and determinism. This collection targets readers seeking philosophical frameworks to live more meaningful, examined lives and understand humanity's fundamental questions.
