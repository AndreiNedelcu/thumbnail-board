# Summary

This video explores the erased history of Seneca Village, a thriving integrated African American and immigrant community that existed on Manhattan's Upper West Side in the 1800s before being demolished to create Central Park. The narrative contextualizes how New York's black population sought refuge uptown after slavery's gradual abolition (1800–1830) increased racial tensions in lower Manhattan. Andrew Williams purchased land in the 1820s, sparking community growth that eventually included nearly 300 residents—laborers, domestic workers, and property owners who built homes, churches, and a school. The community became notably integrated, with interracial families and shared civic spaces. However, as New York City's elite sought to establish a grand public park modeled on European parks, they targeted the 750-acre area containing Seneca Village and other settlements housing 1,600 people. Media coverage disparaged residents as living in shanties, masking the community's actual prosperity. Archaeological excavations in 2011 uncovered artifacts and census records revealing Seneca Village as a working and middle-class neighborhood with significant property ownership and educational attainment. Despite residents' legal objections, the city seized and demolished the settlement in 1856, replacing it with Central Park's iconic pathways, bridges, and landscaping. The video contextualizes this as part of a broader pattern of displacement affecting marginalized communities.
