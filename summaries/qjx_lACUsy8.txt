# Summary

This video explains how modern money supply is created and why most of the world's money is not physically printed. The presenter traces the historical evolution from gold-backed currency through the Bretton Woods system to today's fiat money, explaining how three historical accidents—Archduke Franz Ferdinand's assassination, World War I's inflation, and Richard Nixon's suspension of the gold standard in 1971—shaped modern monetary systems. The core explanation focuses on how banks create money through lending: when a bank issues a loan, it creates new digital currency as both an asset (the loan to you) and a liability (their promise to pay you), doubling the money supply temporarily. This principle applies to both commercial banks lending to individuals for mortgages and central banks partnering with governments to finance public projects through bonds. The video emphasizes that 80-95% of money creation happens through commercial bank lending rather than physical printing, and that most money disappears when debts are repaid—banks profit only from interest charges. This system remains largely misunderstood by the general public, making it an ideal topic for financially curious viewers seeking to understand macroeconomic mechanisms.
